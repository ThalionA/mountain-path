Three broad behavioural patterns that are seen in all humans across cultures:
- Conformism
	- Seen in very young children already 
	- Sometimes is driven by the need to acquire skills
	- Often it's driven by the need to belong to a group
	- Over-imitation gets stronger when it does *not* have an instrumental value (i.e. learning an action)
	- Conformism decreases as the population of a society gets larger, since strangers have smaller effects on our lives 
	- Rituals are a way to increase conformism
- Religiosity
	- Babies already pay more attention to "supernatural" beings (breaking intuitive laws of physics), expect them to win in confrontations with "normal" beings
	- Organised religions have their roots in ritualised actions that were connected to agriculture. The more often and the more routinised these rituals become, the more people come to accept whatever is represented with the rituals
	- Bidirectional relationship between rituals and agriculture (each supports the other)
		- Both also support future-minded thinking
	- Organised religions were a way to increase conformism and coherence as societies got larger
	- It was relatively hard for leaders to assume the role of an actual god, because extraordinary claims would attract scepticism etc
- Tribalism
	- Shared experiences (especially suffering) lead to closer bonds and "identity fusion" with other of a group (tribe, football fans, band members etc)
		- In traditional tribes this was ensured with initiation rituals, including painful genital mutilation, bullet ants in a glove
		- The more painful and scary the experience, the more the members identify with and are ready to fight and die for the group
	- Smaller (and less complex, less stratified) societies rely on more infrequent, more powerful rituals to achieve identity fusion. Larger societies rely on more frequent, less impactful rituals.
	- Human sacrifice (mostly of children, slaves, lower classes) appears as societies get more complex and unequal, but after a "threshold" (of around 1 million people) it is less prominent, because these kinds of societies become brittle. It was a show of force by the all-powerful leader, but also a way to appease the capricious gods so that they wouldn't visit destruction, famine etc to the people.  
		- Ripping out hearts by the Incas..
	- Larger groups, e.g. nations, cannot rely on identity fusion as easily, because it only extends to members of a group with shared experiences. However, larger groups can still have "identification". Identification differs from fusion in that in-group members are not as likely to fight and die for each other, but are more likely to be hostile to out-groups even without a threat/conflict.
