> [!note] About these notes
> Listened to as audiobook. Made me think of how grateful I am to be living in this era — see the related [[../musings/gratitude|Gratitude]] musing. I'd place it in the same category as Sapiens and [[guns-germs-and-steel|Guns, Germs, and Steel]].

- Enlightenment values (reason, science, humanism, progress) are relevant now as much as ever.
- The general point of the book is not to self-congratulate, but to find out the reasons behind the progress we've witnessed over the past couple of centuries, and do more of what helps and less of what doesn't.
- Three crucial concepts: entropy, evolution, information.
- People are optimistic when it comes to their own circumstances (both present and future), but pessimistic when it comes to more global assessments (e.g. my own financial situation will improve, but the country's is in decline; my own neighbourhood is safe, but the city is getting worse). This is called the *optimism gap*.
- The *availability bias*, coupled with the tendency of news outlets to over-represent negative events (because they "sell" more), drives people to a bleaker outlook on the present and future, compared to the past. The world has been ending for a long time.
- It's easy to be a pessimist, and being one makes people take you more seriously.
- "How many things can happen today that can make you much better off? How many things can happen that could make you much worse off?"
- Barack Obama: "If you were to choose one time to be born, without knowing a priori which place, which class, which race, etc you would belong to, you would have to choose now."
- Average life expectancy has skyrocketed — from below 40 even in the West to above 70 worldwide (above 80 in some countries). Infant mortality and maternal mortality during childbirth have fallen massively.
- Germ theory, antibiotics, chlorination of water, vaccines, mosquito control, and other discoveries/inventions have led to much lower numbers of deaths from disease (including complete eradication of some, like smallpox).
- Advances in technology, specialisation, and genetic modification of crops have led to higher and less variable crop yields. Fewer people are subject to hunger and famine. The average calorie intake per person has risen. (In the past, this was a major problem — people resorted to cannibalism in times of famine.)
- As countries get richer, they also get happier. As new wealth is generated, inequality usually rises, followed by a decrease — an inverted-U shape. As countries get richer they also spend more on social causes (education, healthcare). Poverty is more important to alleviate than inequality. Events that decrease inequality usually involve mass death and destruction.
- Even adjusting for inflation isn't quite fair, because today we have access to things no amount of money or power could have bought in the past (a fridge, antibiotics, a phone in your pocket).
- The best system we have so far for generating wealth is democracy and free markets. Authoritarian regimes and communist systems have made their people worse off (centralised control, reduced innovation, isolation).
- Wealthier nations care more about environmental causes, recycling, etc. CO2 emissions go through an inverted-U as countries develop.
- Humans have never exhausted a natural resource before moving on to different ones — scarcity drives higher prices and innovation. "The stone age didn't end because we ran out of stones."
- *Dematerialisation* (replacing things with counterparts that use less material or just information — less material in cans, smaller computers, books to ebooks, vinyls to Spotify) contributes to decreasing resource use.
- Agriculture actually released a lot of CO2, because of plowing the land. There is no "natural" farming, because to have crops you have to clear the land of every other living being, maintain a monoculture, select species based on usefulness to humans, fertilise, use pesticides.
- Temperatures have risen by 0.8 °C, and could reach 1.5 °C. We should not pass 2, and absolutely must not reach 4.
- Oil spills have decreased, even though more oil is transferred. Rainforest deforestation has decreased. Energy efficiency (per unit CO2) gets better as you go from wood → coal → oil → gas, because of the carbon:hydrogen ratio.
- We have to "internalise costs" so corporations have to account for environmental effects (carbon tax). Otherwise it becomes a *tragedy of the commons*.
- Solar and wind power require a lot of land. The most efficient source of energy by land use is nuclear power. Nuclear is also safer than other options.
- We'd also have to actively de-carbonise the atmosphere. The best known way is via plants, which take up CO2 — but slowly.
- If we don't keep rising temperatures in check, there's a "plan B" of spraying a fine mist of particles (sulfates) in the stratosphere from airplanes, increasing reflection of the sun's rays — similar to volcanic eruptions (a volcano in the Philippines in the 1990s cooled global temperatures by 0.5 °C for 2 years).
- We now live in the era of "The Long Peace" following WWII. Interstate wars have decreased a lot. Some factors contributing to fewer wars: increasing trade between states; increasing democracy (pairs of democracies are less likely to go to war); war is "illegal" (sanctions follow annexation); the destructive power of current weapons is much greater (mutually assured annihilation).
- After the Enlightenment there was a counter-Enlightenment period in the 1800s — Romanticism. War, honour, heroism were idealised (Nietzsche, etc.).
- Non-war homicide and violence has also decreased. Violence is fractal in its distribution — few countries account for most homicides, few cities within those countries, few neighbourhoods, few individuals. The most dangerous countries are in Central and South America, and Southern Sub-Saharan Africa. More affluent countries have fewer homicides. Much violence is associated with drug trafficking. *Severity* of punishment matters less than *certainty and predictability* of punishment.
- Terrorism is a very weak and ineffectual tactic. The only reason we care as much as we do is *because* our lives are so much safer now, and because news covers it disproportionately.
- Fewer people die of accidents of all kinds (apart from drug poisoning) — especially since factories were incentivised to care about worker safety.
- More countries are democratic now. Chaos and state collapse create more misery than autocracies.
- Countries respect human rights and equality more. Less racism, sexism, homophobia over time. Fewer hate crimes. Most oppressive countries are in the Middle East. Most have abolished capital punishment.
- Education has been on the rise. Developing countries now have literacy rates that match those of the developed world a few decades ago. Their young populations have high literacy rates; mostly their elderly populations are illiterate. The least-literate countries are in war-torn / authoritarian regimes (Afghanistan, where under the Taliban it's illegal for women to be educated).
- *Hedonic treadmill* explains why we're so ungrateful about all our modern boons and comforts.
- Suicide rates have gone down overall (though they still increase in relation with crises and wars). They've fallen most for women; stayed relatively stable for men (whose suicide rates are 4x those of women).
- People who vote for right-wing populist leaders/parties generally have lower education (>60% of leave votes from high school graduates, <30% from those with at least a university degree), are not primarily concerned with economics (more about immigration, terrorism), are not from the absolutely lowest classes but from the lower middle class, and are generally older. People tend to take their beliefs along with them as they age, rather than become more conservative. Younger generations are more liberal than previous ones.
