Audiobook. Talks about [[Cognitive biases|cognitive biases]]. 

- Weirdly, people's degree of rationality does not only depend on the type of problem at hand, but also the formulation/content of the problem. It is not just formal logic that matters. For example, people are better able to solve problems if they involve fairness/cheating or danger (presumably, because these are [[Evolution|evolutionarily]] important objectives to keep in mind)
- [[Conjunction fallacy]]
- Humans are never as irrational as when defending their pet ideas. Adversarial collaborations are the answer for science. 
- Some of the fallacies are "optimal" judgements for different contexts than the ones defined in the experiments. They are repurposed responses for evolutionary problems.
- Rationality has to be defined with respect to an agent that pursues certain goals. A rational agent uses acquired knowledge (justified true beliefs) to achieve those goals. 
- Sometimes it is rational to be ignorant (can't be threatened to divulge a secret), to cede control (Odysseus with the sirens, or during negotiations), to be a "madman", which usually we think of as irrational. 
- Valid (conclusions follow from premises) and sound arguments (premises are also true) are not the same. A common fallacy is presenting a valid argument as if it were sound.
- Affirming the consequent is a formal fallacy: from if p then q, concluding that if q then p. 
- Informal fallacies:
	- Strawman fallacy. Presenting the weakest point of the opponent. 
	- "Burden tennis" - throwing the burden of proof to the other person
	- Arguments from authority
	- Bandwagon fallacy
	- Arguing ad hominem - attacking the person
	- Genetic fallacy - caring about where the idea came from not its soundness
	- Appeal to emotion and affective fallacy (rejecting facts because they are hurtful)
- Many concepts do not have necessary and sufficient conditions. Rather they have family similarity by which they are recognised. 
- Independence of different events is very important when calculating probabilities. Falsely assuming independence, but also falsely assuming dependence, lead to serious errors in judgement. 
- The cluster illusion is a form of posthoc fallacy
- Extraordinary claims require extraordinary evidence, because of [[Bayes' rule]]. The more unlikely a hypothesis/finding a priori, the more likely it is to be false, and the greater the burden of proof. 
- Even though predictive accuracy for people's outcomes is higher when taking into account people's sex, race, socioeconomic status etc, it might be good to neglect the base rates to avoid discrimination and self-fulfilling prophecies. 
- When choosing a prior, there's a [[../musings/everything-is-a-trade-off|trade-off]] between specificity (relevance to the matter at hand) and reliability (number of samples from which the prior is calculated). 
- People are better "Bayesians" when probabilities are turned into concrete numbers and even better into colours and shapes which are more intuitively understood. 
- People are risk-averse when it comes to gains (preferring the sure thing), but risk-seeking when it comes to losses (preferring to gamble if it avoids a loss). The framing of a question can make them switch their preference entirely, even when the expected utility of different options remains exactly the same. These type of framing biases can be used to manipulate public opinions, or private decisions (such as surgery vs radiation etc)
- [[Signal Detection Theory]] = [[Probability Theory]] + [[Rational Choice Theory]]
- [[Gaussian distribution|Gaussian distributions]] arise in the world when some variable (e.g. a characteristic of a person, like height etc) is the result of many small (in effect) causes.
- Our moral aspirations for justice (not sentencing innocent people and not acquitting guilty people) really outstrip our current technical ability to evaluate forensic evidence (d' is 1 or so, whereas we'd need 3 or more)
- The "my-side bias" affects people irrespective of education level, political beliefs, etc. Another name for it is "motivated reasoning". Funnily enough, people are *less* susceptible to cognitive biases when it suits their own conclusions, but more so when it does not. 
- Difference between "distal" (about the far past or future, people far away, the micro or macro) and "testable" (about things in every day life) beliefs. People have usually rational beliefs about the "testable" sphere, because these things actually have a bearing on their lives. Not aligning with reality on these would have adverse consequences. Another dichotomy is the "reality mindset" vs the "mythology mindset".
