- "Naïve" view of information: More information is always better, because it leads to truth and wisdom by itself. 
- Information does not always "represent" reality (as an aside, any representation of reality is bound to leave out many parts of it by necessity), but it always connects and brings together in networks
- 3 levels of reality
	- Objective, things that exist whether anyone is aware of them or not
	- Subjective, like pain, pleasure, love etc
	- Inter-subjective, based on shared beliefs and stories
- Mythology and Bureaucracy
	- Both important for societies
	- Mythology is much more easily understood by humans
		- Certain human "dramas" that are evolutionarily relevant (care/abandonment by parents, sibling rivalry, competition for mate, etc) and play out in all popular stories
	- Bureaucracy is not well understood and much despised
	- Order and truth are not always aligned
- Science differs from religious institutions in that it has strong self-correcting mechanisms, and it promotes self-doubt
- Dictatorship - Democracy as a continuum of information flow
	- Dictatorships have information flows into and out of the centre, where all decisions are made
	- In democracies, information is distributed
	- Democracies have self-correcting mechanisms (different parts of the state - legislative, executive, judicial - serve as mutual checks and balances for each other)
	- Autocrats start eroding these self-correcting mechanisms first (like Augustus Caesar and Vladimir Putin), to remove checks on their power, while keeping them in place just for show
- Democracy is not the same as just elections - 99% cannot decide to exterminate the 1% minority with elections
- Democracy requires widely available information, ability to discuss freely, and a basic understanding of the issues
- Stone age communities were democratic, because information was easily accessible to everyone, and everyone was well aware of the issues
	- Tribal leaders could not control information flow among people
	- Also lacked the resources to micromanage every decision
- As societies grew, "democracy" became impossible, because people could not communicate easily and fast enough, and also it was impossible to know about all the issues half a world away
	- However, in the Roman Empire for example (with 10s of millions of citizens) the local cities and communities were still run as democracies
	- In Pompei 79CE (when Vesuvius erupted?), there were graffiti with election campaigns (even slandering each other, like "the drunks/thieves ask you to vote for so and so politician for edile/magistrate")
- Most democracies (Athens, Rome, early US) gave voting rights to very small percentages of the adult population, usually only free affluent men (slaves, women, non-citizens were not given voting rights) - like less than 30% in all cases, even as low as 5%.
- Mass media allowed democracies by giving access to information
- Many politicians rose to power after first working as editors for newspapers, notably Mussolini (was first a socialist??!) and Lenin!
