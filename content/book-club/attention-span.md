> [!note] About these notes
> Listened to as audiobook.

- Ability to focus has decreased over the years (now it's 45 seconds before interruption).
- Multi-tasking makes people feel more stressed.
- Unfinished tasks leave an attentional "residue", taking up resources by staying in our mind even when we're not engaged in them.
- Cognitive load degrades attention, explained through the theory of depletion of cognitive resources.
	- What are these "cognitive resources"? They seem to involve frontal cortex for executive control. Why do they get "depleted"? Is there any evidence for metabolic effects? Does energy availability play a role? Sleep clearly plays a role, but *how*? Are frontal cortical areas more metabolically demanding?
- Loss of sleep is really deleterious.
- Fighting temptations drains cognitive resources.
- To an extent, attention profile is related to personality traits (impulsivity, conscientiousness, neuroticism).
- When in a better mood, people can think of more options to solve a problem.
- Film shots have been getting shorter over time (from more than 12s in the 1920s to less than 4s in 2010).
	- Similar trends in TV.
	- YouTube videos employ many jump cuts to keep the viewer's attention.
	- Music videos have the shortest shot lengths (even below 1s).
	- Ads have also become both shorter and with shorter shot lengths.
- Social media all have limits on content size (duration, number of photos, characters) meant to keep the user's attention.
- Children who have viewed fast-paced videos then struggle to keep attention on a subsequent task.
	- Is it possible to reverse this? Apart from mindfulness practice, are there other interventions?
- We need to increase our agency. An essential part of behaviour change is actually understanding what drives our behaviour and being aware of the behaviours we engage in.
