## Hook
If you feel bad because you haven't been progressing as fast as you'd like in your climbing, and you've set a goal for the new year to break into some boulder or sport grade — this video is for you. Most "fast progress" content on YouTube isn't telling you what you need to know.

The question isn't how fast you can get better. It's why you want to.

## Why fast progress is irrelevant

- **Sisyphus (Camus).** The absurdity of existence — there's no inherent meaning to anything, and much less so in climbing, so we need to create our own.
- **Arrival fallacy.** When high-level climbers complete a long-term project, they just get stuck into the next hard one.
- **Effort justification.** People value more what they have put effort into. This is why we cherish a handmade gift more than a store-bought one, or why we feel a stronger connection to a climb we worked on for weeks versus one we flashed.
- **Conquistadors of the useless.** We're not climbing to survive or to acquire resources. We're doing it for the sheer joy of it. Intrinsic motivation — not a means to an end but an end in itself.
- **Motor learning.** Slower progress in the beginning with proper motor learning principles leads to longer-lasting skills and more generalisation and adaptability. (Variability of practice: short term vs long term.)
- **At lower grades you have more room to improve**, which is itself a source of enjoyment.
- **The curve is logarithmic.** If you climb long enough, most of your time will be spent in the flatter part of the improvement curve. How high and how fast it goes up is mostly down to parameters you can't control — height, weight, ape index, starting age.
- **At higher grades the nuances are richer.** If the point is better movement, you don't know about the nuances before you reach a higher level anyway.
- **First-session love.** Think about your first session and why you fell in love with climbing even though you were probably useless. Somewhere along the way, many of us lose that sense of wonder. We start to focus on what we *can't* do, rather than what we *can* do. What if we could recapture that beginner's mind?

> "The best climber is the one having the most fun." — Alex Lowe

## Why fast progress is deleterious

- **Overtraining.**
- **Injury.** My initial advantage of strength led to injuries. Adaptations to tendons happen more slowly than to muscles.
- **Comparisons, ego-trip.** If the point is comparison, you'll always be unhappy — there's always bigger fish. Especially now, social media exposes you to someone better constantly. Even if you're Adam Ondra, Will Bosi is sending V17s. If you're Will, you haven't yet sent a 9c sport.
- **Culture.** Our culture values external achievement over internal growth.
- **Nothing to learn from outliers.** Their progress is usually down to factors that you cannot replicate.
- **Short-term thinking.** Leads to looking for "hacks" and shortcuts where none exist, disregarding the proper training principles. Consistency is king. Your energy is better spent on building a solid foundation of technique and strength.

## When fast progress *might* be relevant

- If you aspire to be a pro athlete or need to get a sponsor.
- If you're an influencer wanting to get clicks (outliers and extremes get attention).
- When you have limited time for a trip or a specific goal — if you're in a place for 2 weeks and want to send a certain climb before you leave.

## Outro

Practical advice:
- Journaling your projects to notice improvement.
- Filming yourself so that you can look back and notice improvement.

The question I'd leave you with: **what if you were stuck at the same grade forever? Would you keep climbing?**
