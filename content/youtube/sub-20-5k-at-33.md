## Hook

For a long time, I had tried and failed to run a sub-20min 5k, until just about a year ago, when I stopped trying altogether, and that's when I finally managed to break through the barrier. There were 2 specific changes I made in my training that led to that breakthrough.

## Context

I'm Theo. I'm a postdoctoral neuroscience researcher, so my job is usually pretty sedentary. I'm 165cm tall and usually weigh between 67–71kg — on the short and stocky side, not really the ideal body type for a runner. I've always played football and run sometimes for fun outside it. These days I play football once a week, and I climb between 3 and 5 days a week. I do one session per week on bench press, deadlifts, and squats, and that's about it for weightlifting (though I do some weighted pullups during my climbing warm-up).

I only started getting interested in running a faster 5k when I got my first Garmin around 2019. During lockdown, since I couldn't play football or climb, I was running much more, and I got pretty close to a sub-20min 5k by running one in 20:36. I didn't follow a specific plan — I just ran a lot, usually distances between 5 and 15km. However, I was making a pretty basic error: I was running relatively fast every time, but not fast enough to really push my limits. I also never ran slow enough to be in the lower heart rate zones and build up aerobic capacity.

In 2023, while living in Cambridge in preparation for an Everest Base Camp trek, I did include some long runs at low intensity — but that was just one run per week, the other being a fast 5k.

## What actually changed

### 1. Lots of low-intensity steady-state aerobic work

After moving back to Edinburgh, I started cycling to work — more than 25km round trip with some elevation thrown in. This meant cycling somewhere between 4 to 8 hours per week, mostly in heart-rate zones 1–2.

I believe this is the first major factor that changed things for me. For a lot of people just getting into running, this training would have to be done by running at a slow enough pace to stay in the low heart rate zones — because they'd also need to get their bodies used to the mechanics of running. For me, cycling worked because I already had a decent running form, but I was lacking longer periods of low-intensity training. So cycling gave me a larger aerobic base at low enough intensities that it didn't tax my system unnecessarily.

### 2. A small dose of very high-intensity hill running

The second major change: I had access to hills again. Cambridge is really flat. But Edinburgh is close to the Pentland hills — no mountains, but enough trail and elevation to do real hill work.

My one dedicated run per week became a trail run in the hills. Usually 5–10km, with about 300m of elevation or more, lasting around 35min to an hour. I didn't try to hold any specific pace — definitely no pace resembling a 5k pace. In the first weeks I had my hands full just running continuously up to the top. Heart rate was usually zone 4, above 170 for the uphills, above 180 towards the top. So these were *very* intense runs, but critically they represented a small portion of my total aerobic training — something like 10–20%.

What these runs gave me:
1. The necessary training in high intensities, even though the pace was slow.
2. Some specific leg strength and muscle endurance.
3. The mental resilience to suffer continuously during hard running efforts.

## Small things that helped at the margins

- **Weight on the lower side** — around 67kg on the day of the run. Probably a minor role.
- **Already warmed up.** Since I cycled to the start point, I was warm before I started. Previously the cycle there was only 5min; now it was 30min.
- **Football duration** went from about 1h to more than 1.5h per game — extra aerobic load.

## Recap

The two accidental changes that unlocked my sub-20 5k were:
1. Building a larger aerobic base with lots of low-intensity cycling.
2. Adding a small dose of very high-intensity hill running to build power and mental toughness.

That's it. It's essentially the advice you'll hear everywhere to make your runs faster. The only thing notable about my version is that it happened accidentally, because I wasn't really training for a faster 5k at that point. The fact that the same prescription works whether or not you're consciously aiming for it is probably worth taking seriously.
